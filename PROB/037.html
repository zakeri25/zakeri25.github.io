Suppose the holomorphic germs $f(z)=\lambda z+O(z^2)$ and $g(z)=\nu z+O(z^2)$ are quasiconformally conjugate near the origin. If $|\lambda|=1$, then $\lambda=\nu$.

Use the measurable Riemann mapping theorem to construct a holomorphic family $\{ g_t \}_{|t|<1+\ve}$ of quasiconformally conjugate maps with $g_0=f$ and $g_1$ conformally conjugate to $g$.

Let $h$ be a quasiconformal homeomorphism defined near $0$ which satisfies $h(0)=0$ and $h \circ f =g \circ h$. Consider the Beltrami form $\mu=h^{\ast} \mu_0$, where $\mu_0$ is the zero Beltrami form corresponding to the standard conformal structure of $\CC$. The form $\mu$ is defined near the origin and is clearly $f$-invariant. Choose a small enough $\delta>0$ and extend $\mu$ to $\CC$ by setting $\mu(z)=0$ for $|z|>\delta$. Consider the Beltrami forms $t \mu$ for $|t|<1/\|\mu\|_{\infty}$. Since $f^{\ast} \mu =\mu$ and $f^{\ast}$ acts as a rotation on Beltrami forms, it follows that $f^{\ast}(t\mu)=t\mu$ near $0$. Let $h_t: \CC \to \CC$ be the unique solution of the Beltrami equation $h_t^{\ast} \mu_0 =t\mu$ normalized to fix $0,1$. Then $g_t:= h_t \circ f \circ h_t^{-1}$ is a $1$-quasiconformal homeomorphism near $0$, hence holomorphic there. Moreover, $t \mapsto g_t(z)$ is holomorphic for each fixed $z$ sufficiently close to $0$ [Caution: This is true but needs to be checked: even though $h_t$ depends holomorphically on $t$, the inverse $h_t^{-1}$ may not]. Writing $g_t(z)=\lambda_t \, z + O(z^2)$, it follows that $t \mapsto \lambda_t$ is also holomorphic. But $g_t$ is conjugate to $f$ whose fixed point at $z=0$ is indifferent, so $|\lambda_t|=1$ for $|t|<1/\|\mu\|_{\infty}$. The open mapping theorem now implies that $t \mapsto \lambda_t$ is constant. Since $h_0=\text{id}$, we have $g_0=f$ so $\lambda_0=\lambda$. Similarly, $h_1 \circ h^{-1}$ is conformal, so $g_1$ is holomorphically conjugate to $g$, so $\lambda_1=\nu$. We conclude that $\lambda=\nu$.

Remark. The condition $|\lambda|=1$ is necessary; for example, the linear maps $f(z)=2z$ and $g(z)=3z$ are quasiconformally conjugate by $h(z)=|z|^{\log 3/\log 2-1}z$. The conclusion of this problem is true even if the conjugacy $h$ is merely a homoemorphism. This highly non-trivial statement was first proved in V. Naishul, Topological invariants of analytic and area-preserving mappings and their application to analytic differential equations in $\CC^2$ and $\CC {\mathbb P}^2$, Trans. Moscow Math. Soc., 2 (1983) 239-250. For a different, more transparent proof, see R. Perez-Marco, Fixed points and circle maps, Acta Math., 179 (1997) 243-294.
