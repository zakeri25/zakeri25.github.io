In the picture, $|AB|=3|AF|$, $|BC|=3|BD|$ and $|CA|=3|CE|$. Show that $$ \area(\triangle ABC)=7\area(\triangle PQR). $$

Either explore the relative areas of various triangles in the picture using elementary geometry, or use vectors.

Geometric solution: For simplicity let us write $\ov{ABC}$ for $\area(\triangle ABC)$ etc. Let $x:=\ov{AFR}$. Evidently $\ov{BFR}=2x$ and $\ov{ARC}=2\ov{ARB}=6x$, from which it follows that $\ov{ABC}=3\ov{AFC}=21x$. The same argument now shows that $\ov{BDP}=\ov{CEQ}=x$ and therefore $\ov{FBPR}=\ov{DCQP}=\ov{EARQ}=5x$. Thus, $$ \ov{PQR}= 21 x - 18 x = 3x = \frac{1}{7} \ov{ABC}. $$ Algebraic solution: Let $A$ be the origin and identify each point with the vector from $A$ to that point. Then $D=(2B+C)/3$, $E=2C/3$, $F=B/3$. The point $P$ is the intersection of $BE$ and $AD$, so there are $0 < t,s < 1$ such that $$ (1-t)B+tE=(1-s)A+sD \Longrightarrow (1-t)B+\frac{2t}{3}C=\frac{2s}{3}B+\frac{s}{3}C. $$ Since $B,C$ are linearly independent, we must have $1-t=2s/3$ and $2t=s$, from which it follows that $t=3/7, s=6/7$. The same argument applied to $Q,R$ will produce identical ratios of segments, so $BP,PQ,QE$ divide $BE$ in the ratios $3/7,3/7,1/7$ and similarly for the segments along $AD$ and $CF$. Taking cross products we obtain \begin{align*} (P-Q) \times (P-R) & = \frac{3}{7} (B-E) \times \frac{3}{7} D \\ & = \frac{9}{49} \Big( B-\frac{2}{3}C \Big) \times \Big( \frac{2}{3}B+\frac{1}{3}C \Big) \\ & = \frac{9}{49} \Big(\frac{1}{3}+\frac{4}{9} \Big) B \times C = \frac{1}{7} B \times C. \end{align*} The claim follows by taking the norm of each side.
