Let $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ be distinct points on the circle $\TT=\RR/\ZZ$ and $f: \TT \to \TT$ be an orientation-preserving homeomorphism. Assume $f$ maps the set $\{ x_1+\theta, \ldots, x_n+\theta \}$ to the set $\{ f(x_1)+\theta, \ldots, f(x_n)+\theta \}$ for some $\theta$. Show that $f(x_i+\theta)=f(x_i)+\theta$ for all $1 \leq i \leq n$.

Verify that the commutator $R^{-1} \circ f^{-1} \circ R \circ f$ has rotation number $0$, where $R: x \mapsto x+\theta$ (mod $\ZZ$).

Let $R: x \mapsto x+\theta$ (mod $\ZZ$) denote the rotation by $\theta$. The hypothesis implies that the commutator $$ [R^{-1},f^{-1}]= R^{-1} \circ f^{-1} \circ R \circ f $$ acts on the set $\{ x_1, \ldots, x_n \}$ and therefore has a well-defined combinatorial rotation number $\rho$ on this set which coincides with the rotation number of $[R^{-1},f^{-1}]$ as a circle homeomorphism. Since $f^{-1} \circ R \circ f$ and $R$ have the same rotation number, there must be at least one $x \in \TT$ such that $(f^{-1} \circ R \circ f)(x)=R(x)$. This means $x$ is a fixed point of $[R^{-1},f^{-1}]$, so $\rho=0$. It follows that $[R^{-1},f^{-1}]$ acts as the identity on $\{ x_1, \ldots, x_n \}$.

Remark. The assumption of $f$ being orienation-preserving is necessary. For example, the complex conjugation map $f:x \mapsto -x$ (mod $\ZZ$) with the fixed points $x_1=0$, $x_2=1/2$ sends $\{ x_1+1/4, x_2+1/4 \}$ to itself, but $f(x_1+1/4)=x_2+1/4$ and $f(x_2+1/4)=x_1+1/4$.
