Suppose $P(x) \in \RR[x]$ satisfies $P(x) \geq 0$ for all $x \in \RR$. Show that $P=A^2+B^2$ for some $A(x),B(x) \in \RR[x]$.

Think of $P$ as an element of $\CC[x]$.

We may assume that $P$ is monic and $\deg(P)=2n>0$. Over the complex field we can factor $P$ as $$ P(z)=\prod_{k=1}^n (z-z_k)(z-\ov{z_k}), $$ where $z_1, \ldots, z_n \in \CC$ are not necessarily distinct (here we have used $P(x) \in \RR[x]$ to assert that non-real roots come in complex conjugate pairs and positivity of $P$ on $\RR$ to conclude that the assertion also holds for real roots, i.e., real roots must have even multiplicities). Decompose $\prod_{k=1}^n (x+iy-z_k) = u(x,y)+iv(x,y)$ into the real and imaginary parts, where $u(x,y),v(x,y) \in \RR[x,y]$. Then $\prod_{k=1}^n (x-iy-\ov{z_k}) = u(x,y)-iv(x,y)$. Setting $A(x)=u(x,0), B(x)=v(x,0)$, it follows that $$ P(x)=(A(x)+iB(x))(A(x)-iB(x))=(A(x))^2+(B(x))^2. $$
