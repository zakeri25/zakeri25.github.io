Let $U \subset (0,+\infty)$ be open and unbounded. Prove that every interval $[a,b] \subset (0,+\infty)$ contains a point $x$ such that $U \cap \{ x, 2x, 3x, \ldots \}$ is infinite.

Use the Baire category theorem.

Consider the closed sets $$ E_n := \{ x \in [a,b] : kx \notin U \ \text{for all} \ k \geq n \}. $$ Suppose the claim is false. Then $[a,b] = \bigcup_{n \geq 1} E_n$, so by the Baire category theorem some $E_n$ has non-empty interior. Take an open interval $I=(c,d)$ in such $E_n$. Since $(k+1)c < kd$ for all large enough $k$, the union $nI \cup (n+1)I \cup (n+2)I \cup \cdots$ contains an interval of the form $(x_0,+\infty)$. By definition this union is disjoint from $U$, which implies $U \subset [0,x_0]$, contradicting the hypothesis that $U$ is unbounded.
