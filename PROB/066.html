Does there exist a continuous function $f: \RR \to \RR$ which satisfies $f(f(x))=\cos x$ for all $x \in \RR$?

The answer is no. Look at a neighborhood of the unique fixed point of the cosine function.

It is easy to see that the cosine function has a unique fixed point $0 < p < \pi/2$. Suppose $f$ satisfies the given condition, so $p$ is the unique fixed point of $f \circ f$. If $f(p) \neq p$, by continuity one of the inequalities $f(x) > x$ or $f(x) < x$ should hold for all $x$. But then $\cos x = f(f(x)) > f(x) > x$ or $\cos x = f(f(x)) < f(x) < x$ for all $x$, which is a contradiction. Thus, $f(p)=p$ is the unique fixed point of $f$. Any zero of $f(x)-p$ is a zero of $\cos x -p$, so $p$ is an isolated zero of $f(x)-p$. Looking at the four possibilities for the sign of $f(x)-p$ immediately before and after $p$, we see that the sign of $f(f(x))-p$ either changes from negative to positive or does not change at all. But the sign of $\cos x -p$ changes from positive to negative near $p$. This contradiction proves that no such $f$ can exist.

Remark. It would be much easier to prove the non-existence of such $f$ under the additional assumption of differentiability at $p$. Simply notice that $(f \circ f)'(p)=(f'(p))^2 \geq 0$ whereas $\cos' p = -\sin p = - \sqrt{1-p^2}<0$.
