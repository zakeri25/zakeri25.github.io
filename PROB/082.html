Let $p(z)$ be a complex polynomial of degree $n$ having all its roots on the unit circle $|z|=1$. Let $q(z)=p(z)/z^{n/2}$. Show that all roots of $q'(z)$ are also on the unit circle.

Start with the fact that if $q'(c) =0$ and $p(c) \neq 0$, then $cp'(c)/p(c)=n/2$.

It suffices to show that if $q'(c)=0$ and $p(c) \neq 0$, then $|c|=1$. Differentiating $z^n q^2(z)=p^2(z)$ gives \begin{align*} \Longrightarrow & \ n z^{n-1} q^2(z) + 2 z^n q(z) q'(z) = 2 p(z) p'(z) \\ \Longrightarrow & \ n c^{n-1} q^2(c) = 2 p(c) p'(c) \\ \Longrightarrow & \ n p^2(c)=2c \, p(c) p'(c) \\ \Longrightarrow & \ c p'(c)/p(c) = n/2. \end{align*} Writing $p(z)=\con \prod_{j=1}^n (z-a_j)$ where $|a_j|=1$ for all $j$, we obtain $$ \frac{n}{2} = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{c}{c-a_j}. $$ Summing the relations $1-c/(c-a_j)=-a_j/(c-a_j)$ over $j$ then gives $$ \frac{n}{2} = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{-a_j}{c-a_j} \Longrightarrow -\frac{n}{2} = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\ov{a_j}}{\ov{c}-\ov{a_j}}. $$ It now follows from $|a_j|=1$ that $$ 0 = \sum_{j=1}^n \left[ \frac{c}{c-a_j}+\frac{\ov{a_j}}{\ov{c}-\ov{a_j}} \right] = (|c|^2-1) \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{|c-a_j|^2} $$ which proves $|c|=1$.
