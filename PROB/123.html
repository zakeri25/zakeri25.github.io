Let $\mathcal P$ be the vector space of all real polynomials on $\RR$ and $T: {\mathcal P} \to \RR$ be a linear functional with the property that $T(fg)=0$ implies $T(f)T(g)=0$. Prove that there exist $a,c \in \RR$ such that $T(f)=cf(a)$ for all $f \in {\mathcal P}$.

Consider the numbers $c_n=T(x^n)$. Discuss the cases $c_0=0$ and $c_0 \neq 0$ separately.

Set $c_n:=T(x^n)$ for $n \geq 0$. First suppose $c_0=0$. We show that $c_n=0$ for all $n \geq 0$ and therefore $T \equiv 0$, so the claim holds for $c=0$ and arbitrary $a$. To see this, suppose $c_n \neq 0$ for some $n$, so $c_{2n}=T(x^n x^n) \neq 0$. Set $\lambda= c_{2n}/c_n$. Then $T(x^n(x^n-\lambda))=0$, hence $T(x^n-\lambda)=0$. This gives $c_n-\lambda c_0=c_n=0$, a contradiction.

Now suppose $c_0 \neq 0$. Replacing $T$ with $T/c_0$ we may assume $c_0=1$. We show that the claim holds for $c=1$ and $a=c_1$. This would follow once we check that $c_n = {c_1}^n$ for all $n \geq 1$. We consider two cases:

Case I. $c_1=0$. We need to check that $c_n=0$ for all $n \geq 2$. Suppose $c_n \neq 0$ for a minimal $n \geq 2$ and set $\lambda=-c_n$. Then $T((x+\lambda)(x+1)^{n-1})=T(x^n+\lambda)=0$. Since $T(x+1)=1$, we have $T((x+1)^{n-1}) \neq 0$ and therefore $T(x+\lambda)=0$. But this implies $\lambda=0$, a contradiction.

Case II. $c_1 \neq 0$. Then $c_n \neq 0$ for all $n \geq 0$. We prove $c_n = {c_1}^n$ by induction on $n$, the case $n=1$ being trivial. Assume the formula holds for some $n \geq 1$. Since $T(x^n(c_{n+1}- c_n x))=T(c_{n+1} x^n - c_n x^{n+1})=0$, we have $T(c_{n+1}- c_n x)=0$. This gives $c_{n+1}-c_nc_1=0$, so by the induction hypothesis $c_{n+1}= c_n c_1 = c_1^{n+1}$, completing the induction step.
