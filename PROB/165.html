Suppose $\omega$ is a primitive $q$-th root of unity in $\CC$. Show that the roots of unity in the field $\QQ(\omega)$ are precisely the elements $\pm \omega^j$ for $0 \leq j \leq q-1$, so there are $q$ or $2q$ of them according as $q$ is even or odd.

For any primitive $n$-th root of unity $a \in \CC$, the degree of the cyclotomic extension $\QQ(a)$ over $\QQ$ is $\varphi(n)=\# \{ 1 \leq j \leq n-1: \gcd(j,n)=1 \}$. Use this to show that the roots of unity in $\QQ(\omega)$ form a finite cyclic group.

Let $\Gamma$ be the set of roots of unity in $\QQ(\omega)$. Evidently $\Gamma$ is a subgroup of $\QQ(\omega)$ under multiplication. If $a \in \Gamma$ has order $n$, it follows from $\QQ \subset \QQ(a) \subset \QQ(\omega)$ that $\varphi(n)=[\QQ(a):\QQ] \leq [\QQ(\omega):\QQ]=\varphi(q)$. This puts a uniform bound on $n$, hence on the order of $\Gamma$ since for each $n$ there are finitely many $n$-th roots of unity in $\CC$. Thus, $\Gamma$ is a finite group of some order $N$. Since for every $d$ the equation $x^d-1=0$ has at most $d$ solutions in $\CC$, hence in $\Gamma$, a well-known exercise shows that $\Gamma$ must be cyclic. Let $\lambda$ be a generator for $\Gamma$, a primitive $N$-th root of unity. Then $\omega \in \Gamma$ is a power of $\lambda$, which easily implies $\QQ(\omega)=\QQ(\lambda)$, hence $\varphi(q)=\varphi(N)$. On the other hand, the order $q$ of $\omega$ must divide $N$. Write $N=q^k m$, where $k \geq 1$ and $\gcd(q,m)=1$. Then $ \varphi(N)=\varphi(q^k) \varphi(m)=q^{k-1} \varphi(q) \varphi(m)$. This gives $q^{k-1} \varphi(m)=1$, whose only solutions are $k=1$ and $m=1,2$. Thus, either $N=q$ or $N=2q$ and $q$ is odd. In the first case $\Gamma = \{ \omega^j: 0 \leq j \leq q-1 \}$ and $q$ is necessarily even. In the second case $\Gamma = \{ \pm \omega^j: 0 \leq j \leq q-1 \}$ and $q$ is odd.
