Consider a quadrilateral $ABCD$ (vertices labeled cyclically) in which the diagonals $AC$ and $DB$ are perpendicular. Show that if another quadrilateral $A'B'C'D'$ has the same side lengths as $ABCD$, then its diagonals $A'C'$ and $D'B'$ are also perpendicular.

Express the condition of orthogonality of the diagonals in terms of the side lengths.

Call $x=\overrightarrow{AB}, y=\overrightarrow{BC}, z=\overrightarrow{CD}, w=\overrightarrow{DA}$, so $x+y+z+w=0$. The diagonals are then $x+y=\overrightarrow{AC}$ and $x+w=\overrightarrow{DB}$. We have \begin{align*} (x+y)\cdot (x+w) =0 & \Longleftrightarrow x \cdot x + x \cdot (y+w) + y \cdot w =0 \\ & \Longleftrightarrow x \cdot x - x \cdot (x+z) + y \cdot w =0 \\ & \Longleftrightarrow x \cdot z = y \cdot w \\ & \Longleftrightarrow \| x+z \|^2 - 2 x \cdot z = \| y+w \|^2 - 2 y \cdot w \\ & \Longleftrightarrow \| x \|^2 + \| z \|^2 = \| y \|^2 + \| w \|^2. \end{align*} The latter condition does not change when $ABCD$ is replaced with $A'B'C'D'$.

Remark. Think of a physical model to demonstrate this. Four rigid rods with ends $A$ and $C$ sliding freely on the positive and negative $x$-axis and $B$ on the positive $y$-axis. The hanging end $D$ will always remain on the negative $y$-axis.
