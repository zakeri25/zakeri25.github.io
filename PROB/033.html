Suppose $f,g: (\RR,0) \to (\RR,0)$ are $C^2$-smooth germs with $f'(0)=g'(0)=1$. If $f,g$ are $C^2$-conjugate near the origin, show that $f''(0)=0$ if and only if $g''(0)=0$.

Look at the second derivatives at the origin.

Let $h: (\RR,0) \to (\RR,0)$ be the germ of a $C^2$ diffeomorphism which satisfies $h\circ f=g\circ h$. Differentiating twice gives $$ (h''\circ f)(f')^2+(h'\circ f)f''=(g''\circ h)(h')^2+(g'\circ h)h''. $$ Evaluating both sides at the origin, we obtain $f''(0)=g''(0)h'(0)$.

Remark. This shows, for example, that $x\mapsto x+x^2$ and $x\mapsto x+x^4$ are not $C^2$-conjugate near the origin even though they are topologically conjugate. Are there such examples that are $C^1$-conjugate?
