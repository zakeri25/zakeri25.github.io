Let $G$ be a group of permutations acting on $\{ 1, \ldots, n \}$. Suppose each non-identity element of $G$ has a unique fixed point. Show that all elements of $G$ share a common fixed point.

Look at the stabilizers $G_k = \{ g \in G: g(k)=k \}$ and the orbits of the action of $G$ on $\{ 1, \ldots, n \}$.

The subgroups $G_k = \{ g \in G: g(k)=k \}$ for $1 \leq k \leq n$ cover $G$ and their pairwise intersections are $\{ e \}$. Partition $\{ 1, \ldots, n \}$ into $G$-orbits $O_{k_1}, \ldots, O_{k_m}$. Then \begin{align*} |G|-1 & = \sum_{k=1}^n (|G_k|-1) = \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{j \in O_{k_i}} (|G_j|-1) \\ & = \sum_{i=1}^m |O_{k_i}| (|G_{k_i}|-1) = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{|G|}{|G_{k_i}|} (|G_{k_i}|-1), \end{align*} from which it follows that $$ 1- \frac{1}{|G|} = \sum_{i=1}^m \left( 1-\frac{1}{|G_{k_i}|} \right). $$ The sum on the right has precisely one non-zero term. In fact, if $|G_{k_i}|, |G_{k_j}| \geq 2$ for some $1 \leq i < j \leq m$, then $1- 1/|G| \geq 1/2 + 1/2 = 1$, which is absurd. It follows that there is a unique $i$ for which $G_{k_i}$ is non-trivial, hence $|G_{k_i}|=|G|$.

Question. Can you characterize all subgroups $G \subset S_n$ with this property? Note that by Burnside's lemma, the number $m$ of orbits is $1/|G|$ times the sum over $g \in G$ of the size of the fixed point set of $g$. This gives $|G|(m-1)=n-1$. In particular, if $m=2$ then $|G|=n-1$. The simplest example of this type is when elements of $G$ fix a given point and permute the remaining $n-1$ cyclically, giving rise to a cyclic group $G$. For a non-cyclic example, consider the subgroup $G \subset S_7$ generated by the permutations $g=(1)(234)(567)$ and $h=(1)(25)(37)(46)$. Since $g^3=h^2=e$ and $gh=hg^{-1}$, $G$ is isomorphic to $S_3$.
