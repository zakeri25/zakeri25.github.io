Suppose $f:(0,+\infty) \to (0,+\infty)$ is locally integrable and $\ds{\liminf_{x \to +\infty} f(2x)/f(x) \geq 1}$. Show that $$ \int_1^{\infty} x^{\alpha} f(x) \, dx = +\infty \qquad \text{for every} \ \alpha > -1. $$

Assuming the integral converges for some $\alpha>-1$, take a large $N>0$ and estimate $I=\int_N^{\infty} x^{\alpha} f(x) \, dx$ from below in terms of $I$ itself.

Take an arbitrary $0<\ve<1$ and find $N>0$ such that $f(2x)/f(x)>1-\ve$ whenever $x>N$. Suppose the above integral converges for some $\alpha>-1$. Set $I:=\int_N^{\infty} x^{\alpha} f(x)\, dx$. Then, using the change of variable $x=2t$, we obtain $$ I = 2^{1+\alpha} \int_{N/2}^{\infty} t^{\alpha} f(2t)\, dt \geq 2^{1+\alpha} \int_N^{\infty} t^{\alpha} f(2t)\, dt \geq 2^{1+\alpha} (1-\ve) I, $$ which shows $2^{1+\alpha} (1-\ve) \leq 1$. Letting $\ve \to 0$ then gives $2^{1+\alpha} \leq 1$, which is a contradiction.
