Prove that every bijection of a given set is the composition of two involutions.

Decompose the set into the disjoint union of orbits of the bijection.

Let $f:X \to X$ be a bijection. Decompose $X$ into the disjoint union of full orbits $O(x)=\{ x_n:=f^{\circ n}(x) \}_{n \in \ZZ}$ as $x$ ranges over a suitable subset $S \subset X$ which contains one point from each orbit (this, in general, requires the axiom of choice). Notice that each $O(x)$ is either countably infinite, or finite of length $p$ where $x_n=x_m$ iff $n \equiv m \ (\operatorname{mod} p)$. For each $x \in S$ define the involutions $g,h: O(x) \to O(x)$ by $$ g(x_n)=x_{-n} \quad \text{and} \quad h(x_n)=x_{-n-1} \quad (n \in \ZZ). $$ Evidently $f=g\circ h$ on $O(x)$. The result follows by patching these partial definitions of $g,h$.
