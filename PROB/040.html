You arrive at a road junction with options to turn right or left. Only one choice leads to home. There are three men standing at the junction: $T$ (who always tells the truth), $L$ (who always lies) and $R$ (who randomly tells the truth or lies). These men know each other and know which turn leads to home. How can we find the correct turn by asking only two questions with yes/no answers?

Think of asking the first man a question whose answer would eliminate $R$ for sure.

We pick the first man and ask: "Would you tell people that the second man is $R$?" Here are the possibilities: \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline \text{first man} & T & T & L & L & R & R\\ \hline \text{second man} & L & R & T & R & T & L\\ \hline \text{third man} & R & L & R & T & L & T\\ \hline \text{answer} & \text{No} & \text{Yes} & \text{No} & \text{Yes} & ? & ?\\ \hline \end{array} This shows that if we hear "No" the second man is definitely $T$ or $L$, and if we hear "Yes" the third man is definitely $T$ or $L$. So we pick the corresponding man and ask: "Would you tell people that turning left leads to home?" Here are the possibilities: \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline \text{man} & T & T & L & L\\ \hline \text{correct turn} & \text{left} & \text{right} & \text{left} & \text{right}\\ \hline \text{answer} & \text{Yes} & \text{No} & \text{Yes} & \text{No}\\ \hline \end{array} Evidently in each case we can find the road to home.
