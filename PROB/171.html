Show that a group cannot be the union of two proper subgroups.

The direct proof is simple. Try to find a fancier proof based on the idea of a group acting on two sets.

Suppose $H,K$ are proper subgroups of a group $G$. If $H \subset K$ or $K \subset H$, then clearly $H \cup K \neq G$. Otherwise, take $h \in H \sm K$ and $k \in K \sm H$. Then $hk$ cannot be in $H$ (or else $k \in H$) and it cannot be in $K$ (or else $h \in K$). Hence $hk \notin H \cup K$ and again $H \cup K \neq G$.

For an alternative proof (essentially the same idea but using a fancier formulation, due to J. P. Serre), first note the following: If a group $G$ acts on two sets $X,Y$ and there are points $x \in X$ and $y \in Y$ such that every element of $G$ fixes either $x$ or $y$, then $x$ or $y$ must be fixed by all elements of $G$. Otherwise, there are $g,h \in G$ such that $gx \neq x$ and $hy \neq y$. Thus, by the assumption, $gy=y$ and $hx=x$. But then $(hg)y=hy \neq y$ and $h(gx) \neq hx$ so $(hg)x \neq x$. Having made this observation, let $G$ be the union $H \cup K$ of its subgroups. Then $G$ acts on the coset spaces $X=G/H$ and $Y=G/K$ and each $g \in G$ fixes either $x=eH \in X$ or $y=eK \in Y$. It follows that either $H$ or $K$ is fixed by the action of $G$, so either $H=G$ or $K=G$.

Remark. Of course a group may well be the union of three proper subgroups, as shown by the example of the Klein $4$-group $$ V_4 \cong \ZZ_2 \oplus \ZZ_2 = \{ (0,0), (1,0) \} \cup \{ (0,0), (0,1) \} \cup \{ (0,0), (1,1) \}. $$ In fact, a group is a union of three proper subgroups iff it has a homomorphism onto $V_4$. See M. Bruckheimer, A. Bryan and A. Muir, Groups which are the union of three subgroups, The American Mathematical Monthly 77 (1970) 52–57.
