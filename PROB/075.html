Suppose $A,B \in \M_n(\CC)$ and $AB-BA$ is a linear combination of $A,B$. Show that $A$ and $B$ share an eigenvector.

Let $AB-BA=aA+bB$ with $a,b \in \CC$ and first consider the case where $ab=0$. The general case can be easily reduced to thic case.

Let $AB-BA=aA+bB$ for some $a,b \in \CC$. The result is easy and well known if $a=b=0$, i.e., when $A$ and $B$ commute. First consider the case where $a \neq 0, b=0$. Take an eigenvector $u_0 \neq 0$ for $B$ so $Bu_0=\lambda u_0$ for some $\lambda \in \CC$. The relation $AB-BA=aA$ then shows that $BAu_0=(\lambda-a)Au_0$. If $Au_0=0$, then $u_0$ is a common eigenvector for $A$ and $B$. Otherwise $Au_0$ is an eigenvector for $B$ with the associated eigenvalue $\lambda-a \neq \lambda$. Repeat the above argument now with $Au_0$ in place of $u_0$, and continue. Since $B$ has at most $n$ distinct eigenvalues, this process must eventually stop, that is, there is a smallest $k$ such that $A^{k+1} u_0=0$, so $A^k u_0$ is a common eigenvector for $A$ and $B$. In a similar fashion, we obtain a common eigenvector in the case $a=0, b \neq 0$. Finally, if $a \neq 0, b \neq 0$, let $c:=b/a$ and observe that $$ (A+cB)B-B(A+cB)=AB-BA=aA+bB=a(A+cB), $$ so by the first case above $A+cB$ and $B$, hence $A$ and $B$, share an eigenvector.
