Show that the alternating group $A_n$ is the only subgroup of $S_n$ of index $2$.

Let $H$ be any subgroup of $S_n$ of index $2$. Use the fact that the product of two transpositions is a square to show that $A_n \subset H$.

Suppose $H \subset S_n$ and $[S_n:H]=2$. Then $g^2 \in H$ for every $g \in S_n$. On the other hand, the product of two transpositions is a square: $(ab)(cd)=(acbd)^2$ if $a,b,c,d$ are distinct, and $(ab)(bc)=(acb)^2$. This shows that every element of $A_n$ is a product of squares and therefore $A_n \subset H$.

Alternative solution. The cases $n=3,4$ can be checked directly, so let us assume $n \geq 5$. As before, let $H \subset S_n$ and $[S_n:H]=2$. The bound $|HA_n|=|H| |A_n|/|H \cap A_n| \leq n!$ gives $|H \cap A_n| \geq n!/4$, so the size of this intersection is either $n!/4$ or $n!/2$. But the former implies $H \cap A_n \triangleleft A_n$ which is impossible since $A_n$ is simple.
