Let $d(n)$ denote the maximum dimension of a subspace $V \subset \M_n(\RR)$ such that $V \sm \{ 0 \} \subset \GL_n(\RR)$. Show that $d(n)\leq n$ for all $n$ and $d(n)=1$ if $n$ is odd.

Consider suitable linear combinations of basis vectors in such $V$.

Take any subspace $V \subset \M_n(\RR)$ with $\dim V>n$. Choose linearly independent matrices $A_1, \ldots A_{n+1}$ in $V$. The first rows of these $n+1$ matrices must be linearly dependent in $\RR^n$, so there are scalars $c_1, \ldots, c_{n+1}$, not all zero, such that the first row of the matrix $A=\sum_{j=1}^{n+1} c_j A_j \in V$ is the zero vector. Then $\det A=0$ but $A \neq 0$ by linear independence of the $A_j$. This shows the property $V \sm \{ 0 \} \subset \GL_n(\RR)$ cannot hold.

Now suppose $n$ is odd and $V \subset \M_n(\RR)$ is a subspace with $\dim V>1$. Take linearly independent matrices $A,B \in V$. The function $p(x)=\det(xA+B)$ is a polynomial of degree $n$. In fact, $p(x)/x^n = \det(A+x^{-1}B) \to \det(A) \neq 0$ as $x \to +\infty$. Since $n$ is odd, $p(x)$ has a real root, which means some non-zero combination $xA+B \in V$ fails to be invertible.

Question. Can you find the value of $d(n)$ for all even $n$? Notice that $d(n)=n$ can happen. For example, the matrices $$ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad B= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & \ \ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$ span a $2$-dimensional subspace $V \subset \M_2(\RR)$ with the property $V \sm \{ 0 \} \subset \GL_2(\RR)$, so $d(2)=2$.
