Prove that every linear functional $\phi: \M_n(\RR) \to \RR$ is of the form $\phi(X)=\tr(XA)$ for a unique matrix $A \in \M_n(\RR)$. If, in addition, $\phi$ satisfies the relation $\phi(XY)=\phi(YX)$ for all $X,Y$, show that $\phi(X)=a \tr(X)$ for some $a \in \RR$.

Look at the action of $\phi$ on the standard basis of $\M_n(\RR)$.

Denote by $E_{ij} \in \M_n(\RR)$ the matrix whose $(i,j)$-entry is $1$ and whose other entries are $0$. Let $A=[a_{ij}] \in \M_n(\RR)$ be the matrix whose $(i,j)$-entry is $\phi(E_{ji})$. Then $$ \phi(E_{ji})=a_{ij}=\tr(E_{ji}A). $$ As the $E_{ij}$ form a basis for $\M_n(\RR)$, we must have $\phi(X)=\tr(XA)$ for all $X$. Uniqueness of $A$ is clear from the above relation.

For the second claim, use the relation $$ E_{ij} E_{\ell k} = \begin{cases} 0 & \quad j \neq \ell \\ E_{ik} & \quad j=\ell \end{cases} $$ to conclude that for every $i,k$ $$ a_{k i} = \phi(E_{ik}) = \phi(E_{i1}E_{1k}) = \phi(E_{1k}E_{i1}) = \tr(E_{1k}E_{i1} A) = \begin{cases} 0 & \quad i \neq k \\ a_{11} & \quad i=k. \end{cases} $$ This shows $A$ is a multiple of the identity matrix, and the result follows. Alternatively, one can observe that $\phi$ must vanish on the $(n^2-1)$-dimensional subspace of trace-free matrices in $\M_n(\RR)$ since it is well known that every trace-free matrix is a commutator. Thus, $\phi(X-(1/n)\tr(X)I)=0$ or $\phi(X)=(\phi(I)/n) \tr(X)$.
