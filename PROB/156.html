Suppose $G$ is a finite group and $\phi: G \to G$ is an automorphism that sends more than $3/4$ of the elements of $G$ to their inverses. Show that $G$ is abelian and $\phi(g)=g^{-1}$ for all $g \in G$.

Let $A = \{ g \in G: \phi(g)=g^{-1} \}$ and show that for every $a \in A$ the intersection $aA \cap A$ is contained in the centralizer $Z(a)$.

Set $n:=|G|$ and $A := \{ g \in G: \phi(g)=g^{-1} \}$. For every $a \in A$ we have $aA \cap A \subset Z(a)$. In fact, let $b \in aA \cap A$ and write $b=ax$ with $x \in A$. The relation $$ \phi(ax)=(ax)^{-1}=x^{-1}a^{-1}=\phi(x)\phi(a)=\phi(xa) $$ shows that $ax=xa$. Hence $ab=aax=axa=ba$, or $b \in Z(a)$. Now $$ |Z(a)| \geq |aA \cap A| = |aA| + |A| - |aA \cup A| > \frac{3n}{2}-n = \frac{n}{2}. $$ Since $Z(a)$ is a subgroup of $G$, this implies $Z(a)=G$. Since $a \in A$ was arbitrary, we conclude that $A$ is contained in the center $Z$ of $G$. This shows $|Z|>3n/4$, so $G=Z$ is abelian. It is now easy to see that $A$ is a subgroup of $G$, hence $A=G$.

Remark. The ratio $3/4$ is sharp. In fact, in the multiplicative group $Q_8$ of unit quaternions $\{ \pm1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k \}$, the automorphism $\phi$ defined by $$ \phi: \pm 1 \mapsto \pm 1, \quad \pm i \mapsto \mp i, \quad \pm j \mapsto \mp j, \quad \pm k \mapsto \pm k $$ sends precisely $6$ elements to their inverses. Yet $Q_8$ is non-abelian.
