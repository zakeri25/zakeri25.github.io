Describe natural (i.e., coordinate-free) isomorphisms {vectors} $\leftrightarrow$ {$1$-forms} and {vectors} $\leftrightarrow$ {$2$-forms} in $\RR^3$ equipped with the standard Euclidean structure. Show that under your identifications the cross product of vectors corresponds to the wedge product of $1$-forms.

Think of the dot product and scalar triple product of vectors.

The vector spaces ${\mathcal A}^1(\RR^3)$ and ${\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$ of all $1$-forms and $2$-forms in $\RR^3$ have dimension $3$. For every vector $p \in \RR^3$ define $\omega^1_p \in {\mathcal A}^1(\RR^3)$ by $\omega^1_p(x):=p \cdot x$ and $\omega^2_p \in {\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$ by $\omega_p^2(x,y):=\det(p|x|y)=p \cdot (x \times y)$. Both linear maps $p \mapsto \omega^1_p$ and $p \mapsto \omega_p^2$ have trivial kernels, so they define isomorphisms $\RR^3 \to {\mathcal A}^1(\RR^3)$ and $\RR^3 \to {\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$, respectively. We need to verify the relation $$ \omega_p^1 \wedge \omega_q^1 = \omega_{p \times q}^2 \qquad \text{for all} \ p,q \in \RR^3. $$ Applying both sides to a pair $(x,y) \in \RR^3 \times \RR^3$, we obtain the equivalent vector identity $$ (p \cdot x)(q \cdot y) - (p \cdot y)(q \cdot x) = (p \times q) \cdot (x \times y). \qquad (*) $$ This can be verified directly (tedious!) or by noting that for a given $p,q$ both sides are bilinear skew-symmetric functions of $x,y$, so it suffices to verify $(*)$ when $x,y$ are distinct unit coordinate vectors ${\bf i}, {\bf j}, {\bf k}$. For example, when $x={\bf i}$ and $y={\bf j}$ the identity reduces to $p_1 q_2 - p_2 q_1 = (p \times q) \cdot {\bf k}$ and so on.

Remark. Setting $p=x,q=y$ in $(*)$ gives the Lagrange identity $$ \| p \times q \|^2 = \| p \|^2 \|q \|^2 - (p \cdot q)^2. $$ Also, since the right side of $(*)$ is $((p \times q) \times x)) \cdot y$, it follows that $(*)$ is equivalent to the identity $$ (p \cdot x) q - (q \cdot x) p = (p \times q) \times x. $$
