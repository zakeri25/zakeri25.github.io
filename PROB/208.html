Suppose $\{ a_n \}$ is a sequence of positive numbers such that $\sum a_n$ converges. Show that $\sum (a_1 \cdots a_n)^{1/n}$ converges as well.

Use the AM-GM inequality on $a_1, 2a_2, 3a_3, \ldots, na_n$.

Let $b_n:=(a_1 \cdot a_2 \cdots a_n)^{1/n}$. By the AM-GM inequality, $$ b_n = (n!)^{-1/n} (a_1 \cdot 2a_2 \cdots na_n)^{1/n} \leq (n!)^{-1/n} \dfrac{\sum_{k=1}^n ka_k}{n}. $$ Using Stirling's formula $n! \sim \sqrt{2\pi n} n^n e^{-n}$ we see that $(n!)^{-1/n}$ is asymptotic to $e/n$ as $n \to \infty$. Hence $\sum b_n$ is comparable to $\sum c_n$, where $$ c_n := \frac{1}{n^2} \sum_{k=1}^n ka_k. $$ Now \begin{align*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n & = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k a_k}{n^2} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty ka_k \left(\sum_{n=k}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} \right) \\ & \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty ka_k \left( \frac{\con}{k} \right) = \con \sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k < + \infty. \end{align*}
Remark. The naive use of AM-GM inequality on $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n$ will not work here since it gives the upper bound $b_n \leq (1/n) \sum_{k=1}^n a_k$ which is asymptotic to $\con/n$. A slightly more careful analysis of the above prove, using the inequality $(n!)^{-1/n} \leq e/(n+1)$, shows that $$ \sum_{n=1}^\infty (a_1 \cdots a_n)^{1/n} \leq e \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n. $$ This is known as Carleman's inequality.
