Suppose $f\in L^1[0,1]$ and $g$ is continuous and $1$-periodic on $\RR$. Show that $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 f(x)g(nx) \, dx = \int_0^1 f(x) \, dx \ \int_0^1 g(x) \, dx. $$

Without loss of generality assume $\int_0^1 g =0$. First treat the case where $f$ is continuous.

It suffices to treat the case where $\int_0^1 g =0$. First assume $f$ is continuous. Take $\ve>0$ and find $N \geq 1$ such that $|f(x)-f(y)|<\ve$ whenever $|x-y|<1/N$. Let $n>N$ and consider the partition $x_i=i/n$ of $[0,1]$. Let $s$ be the step function which takes the value $f(x_i)$ on $I_i=[x_{i-1},x_i)$ for $1 \leq i \leq n$. Note that $\int_0^1 |g(nx)| \, dx = \int_0^1 |g(x)| \, dx$ and $\int_{I_i} g(nx) \, dx=(1/n)\int_{i-1}^i g(x)\, dx=0$ for all $i$ by $1$-periodicity. Thus, \begin{align*} \left| \int_0^1 f(x)g(nx) \, dx \right| & \leq \int_0^1 |f(x)-s(x)| \ |g(nx)| \, dx + \left| \int_0^1 s(x) g(nx) \, dx \right| \\ & \leq \ve \int_0^1 |g(nx)| \, dx + \left| \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) \int_{I_i} g(nx) \, dx \right| = \ve \int_0^1 |g(x)| \, dx. \end{align*} This proves $\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 f(x)g(nx) \, dx=0$. For a general $f \in L^1[0,1]$, take any $\ve>0$ and find a continuous function $h$ on $[0,1]$ such that $\| f-h \|_1 <\ve$. In the relation $$ \int_0^1 f(x)g(nx) \, dx = \int_0^1 (f(x)-h(x)) \ g(nx) \, dx + \int_0^1 h(x) g(nx) \, dx $$ the first term on the right has absolute value $\leq \ve \| g \|_{\infty}$ while the second term tends to $0$ as $n \to \infty$ by the special case treated above. It follows that $$ \limsup_{n \to \infty} \left| \int_0^1 f(x)g(nx) \, dx \right| \leq \ve \| g \|_{\infty}. $$ Since $\ve>0$ was arbitrary, we conclude that $\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 f(x)g(nx) \, dx=0$.

Remark. The example $g(x)=e^{2\pi i x}$ is the classical Riemann-Lebesgue lemma in Fourier analysis.
