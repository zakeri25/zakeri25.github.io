Let $f(x)=(\sin x)/x$ for $x>0$. Show that the $n$-th derivative of $f$ satisfies $|D^n f(x)|<1/(n+1)$ for all $x>0$ and all integers $n \geq 0$.

Take $D^{n+1}$ of each side of the relation $\sin x = x f(x)$.

It suffices to show that the function $$ g(x):= x^{n+1} \Big( \frac{1}{n+1} \pm D^nf(x) \Big) $$ satisfies $g(x)>0$ for all $x>0$. First note that $f$, hence $g$, is $C^{\infty}$-smooth (in fact real-analytic) on the real line. Taking the $(n+1)$-st derivative of each side of $\sin x = x f(x)$ gives \begin{align*} D^{n+1} \sin x &= \sum_{j=0}^{n+1} {n+1 \choose j} D^j(x) D^{n+1-j} f(x) \\ & = x D^{n+1}f(x)+ (n+1) D^n f(x). \end{align*} It follows that \begin{align*} Dg(x) & = x^n \pm (n+1)x^n D^nf(x) \pm x^{n+1} D^{n+1}f(x) \\ & = x^n (1 \pm (n+1) D^nf(x) \pm x D^{n+1}f(x)) \\ & = x^n (1 \pm D^{n+1}\sin x) \end{align*} and the latter is strictly positive for all $x>0$ except the isolated points where $D^{n+1}\sin x = \mp 1$. Since $g(0)=0$, it follows that $g(x)>0$ for $x>0$.
