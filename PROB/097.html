Suppose $f: \RR \to (0,+\infty)$ is a twice differentiable function which satisfies $f f'' \geq 2 (f')^2$ everywhere. Prove that $f$ is constant.

Show that either of the assumptions $f'(a)>0$ or $f'(a)<0$ for some $a \in \RR$ would lead to a contradiction. The auxiliary function $f'/f$ may be useful.

First suppose $f'(a)>0$ for some $a \in \RR$. Then $f'>0$ on $[a,+\infty)$ since by the given inequality $f'' \geq 0$. The function $g:=f'/f$ is positive on $[a,+\infty)$ and satisfies $$ g' = \frac{f f'' - (f')^2}{f^2} \geq \frac{(f')^2}{f^2} = g^2. $$ Thus, for every $x>a$, $$ \int_a^x \frac{g'(t)}{g^2(t)} \, dt \geq \int_a^x dt \Longrightarrow -\frac{1}{g(x)}+\frac{1}{g(a)} \geq x-a \Longrightarrow \frac{1}{g(x)} \leq a+\frac{1}{g(a)} - x. $$ This is a contradiction since the right hand side is negative for large $x$. Similarly (for example by considering the function $x \mapsto f(-x)$), we see that the assumption $f'(a)<0$ for some $a$ leads to a contradiction, and we conclude that $f'=0$ everywhere.
