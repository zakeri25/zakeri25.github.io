Suppose $A \in \M_3(\RR)$ and $Ax$ is orthogonal to $x$ for all $x \in \RR^3$. Prove that there is a vector $p \in \RR^3$ such that $Ax=p \times x$ for all $x$.

Use the fact that $A$ is skew-symmetric.

We have $x^t A x =0$ for all $x$. Hence, for every $x,y$, $$ (x+y)^t A \, (x+y) = x^t A y + y^t A x =0 \Longrightarrow x^t A y = - x^t A^t y. $$ This shows $A^t=-A$ and therefore $A = \begin{bmatrix*}[r] 0 & a & b \\ -a & 0 & c \\ -b & -c & 0 \end{bmatrix*}$ for some $a,b,c \in \RR$. It is now easy to check that the vector $p=\begin{bmatrix*}[r] -c \\ b \\ -a \end{bmatrix*}$ will do.

Alternative solution. This is a more intrinsic view of what is happening. The vector space ${\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$ of all $2$-forms on $\RR^3$ has dimension $3$. Every vector $p \in \RR^3$ defines $\omega_p \in {\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$ by $\omega_p(x,y)=\det(p|x|y)=\langle p \times x, y \rangle$. The linear map $p \mapsto \omega_p$ has trivial kernel, so it must be an isomorphism $\RR^3 \to {\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$. On the other hand, since $A$ is skew-symmetric, $\omega(x,y)=\langle Ax, y \rangle$ belongs to ${\mathcal A}^2(\RR^3)$, hence $\omega=\omega_p$ for some $p \in \RR^3$.
