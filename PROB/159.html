Give three short proofs for the Cayley-Hamilton theorem using topology, complex analysis, and pure algebra: If $A \in \M_n(\CC)$ has the characteristic polynomial $f_A(t)=\det(tI-A)$, then $f_A(A)=0$.

Topology: The statement holds in an open dense set of matrices. Complex analysis: Use Cauchy's integral formula to express $f_A(A)$ as the contour integral of $\operatorname{adj}(zI-A)$ along a large circle. Algebra: Use the fact that if $p(t)$ and $q(t)$ are polynomials with coefficients in $\M_n(\CC)$ and the coefficients of $q$ commute with $A$, then $(p(t)q(t))\Big|_{t=A}=p(A)q(A)$.

First proof (topology). The theorem is trivial if $f_A$ has distinct roots so $A$ is diagonalizable. Since the assignment $A \mapsto f_A(A)$ is continuous, the general case follows once we check that the set of matrices with distinct eigenvalues is dense in $\M_n(\CC)$. To see this, note that the discriminant of $f_A$ and $f'_A$ depends polynomially on the entries of $A$, so it can be thought of as a polynomial map $D: \M_n(\CC) \cong \CC^{n^2} \to \CC$. The zero-set $D^{-1}(0)$ is thus an algebraic variety of dimension $< n^2$ and the complement $\M_n(\CC) \sm D^{-1}(0)$ is open and dense.

Second proof (complex analysis). This is a clever use of Cauchy's integral formula (see Charles McCarthy, The Cayley-Hamilton Theorem, Amer. Math. Monthly, 82 (1975), 390-391). For any polynomial $p(x) \in \CC[x]$ and any $R>\| A \|$, $$ p(A) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{|z|=R} p(z) \, (zI-A)^{-1} \, dz. $$ [This does not require knowledge of functional calculus since we can easily verify it for $p(z)=z^k$: \begin{align*} \int_{|z|=R} z^k \, (zI-A)^{-1} \, dz & = \int_{|z|=R} z^{k-1} \, (I-z^{-1}A)^{-1} \, dz \\ & = \int_{|z|=R} z^{k-1} \, (I+z^{-1}A+z^{-2}A^2+ \cdots) \, dz \\ & = 2\pi i A^k \end{align*} by the residue theorem.] When $p(z)=f_A(z)=\det(zI-A)$, this gives $$ f_A(A) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_{|z|=R} \operatorname{adj}(zI-A) \, dz, $$ which vanishes since the entries of $\operatorname{adj}(zI-A)$ are polynomials in $z$.

Third proof (algebra). This is perhaps the shortest and most elegant proof and it works for any field $F$ (see Peter Lax, Linear Algebra, 2nd ed., Wiley, 2007). For any pair of polynomials $p(t)=\sum_{i=0}^a P_i \, t^i$ and $q(t) = \sum_{j=0}^b Q_j \, t^j$ in $\M_n(F)[t]$, $$ r(t)=p(t)q(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{a+b} R_k \, t^k, \quad \text{where} \quad R_k = \sum_{i+j=k} P_i \, Q_j. $$ If the coefficients of $q$ commute with $A$, it follows that $r(A)=p(A)q(A)$. The choice $q(t)=tI-A$ and $p(t)=\operatorname{adj}(tI-A)$ with $r(t)=\det(tI-A)I=f_A(t)I$ thus leads to $f_A(A)I=p(A)q(A)=0$, from which it follows that $f_A(A)=0$.
