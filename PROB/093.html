Take a sequence $\{ p_n \}$ of distinct points in the open interval $(0,1) \subset \RR$. For each $n \geq 1$ let $\ell_n$ denote the maximum length of the $n+1$ connected components of $(0,1) \sm \{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$. Show that $\limsup_{n \to \infty} n \ell_n \geq 1/\log 2$.

Sort the lengths of the $n+1$ components of $(0,1) \sm \{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$ as $u_1 \leq \cdots \leq u_{n+1}$. The crucial observation is that adding the next few points to the collection $\{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$ will leave most components of $(0,1) \sm \{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$ intact. This should give you an upper bound for the $u$'s in terms of the $\ell$'s.

We may assume $s:=\limsup_{n \to \infty} n \ell_n < +\infty$ since otherwise there is nothing to prove. Take any constant $c > s$, so $\ell_n < c/n$ for all large $n$. Arrange the lengths of the $n+1$ components of $(0,1) \sm \{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$ as $u_1 \leq \cdots \leq u_{n+1}$. For each $1 \leq j \leq n$ the $j$ points $p_{n+1}, \ldots, p_{n+j}$ fall into at most $j$ distinct components of $(0,1) \sm \{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$, so adding them to $\{ p_1, \ldots, p_n \}$ will leave at least $n-j+1$ of these components intact. This shows $\ell_{n+j} \geq u_{n-j+1}$. Hence $$ 1 \leq \sum_{j=1}^n u_{n-j+1} \leq \sum_{j=1}^n \ell_{n+j} < \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{c}{n+j} = \frac{c}{n} \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{1+(j/n)}. $$ Letting $n \to \infty$, we obtain $$ \frac{1}{c} \leq \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{1+(j/n)} = \int_0^1 \frac{dx}{1+x} = \log 2. $$ Since this holds for every $c>s$, we obtain $s \geq 1/\log 2$.
