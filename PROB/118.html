Let $f:[0,1] \to \RR$ be continuous and define the sequence $f_n$ inductively by $$ f_1=f, \quad f_n(x)=\frac{1}{x} \int_0^x f_{n-1}(t)\, dt \qquad (n\geq 2). $$ Show that $f_n \to f(0)$ uniformly on $[0,1]$ as $n \to \infty$.

First treat the case where $f$ is a polynomial with $f(0)=0$.

Evidently each $f_n$ extends continuously to $x=0$, with $f_n(0)=f(0)$. By replacing $f$ with $f-f(0)$ we may assume without loss of generality that $f(0)=0$. First consider the special case where $f(x)$ is a polynomial $\sum_{j=1}^d c_j x^j$. We have $$ f_2(x)=\frac{1}{x} \sum_{j=1}^d c_j \left(\int_0^x t^j \, dt \right) = \sum_{j=1}^d \frac{c_j}{j+1} \, x^j, $$ Inductively, $f_n(x)=\sum_{j=1}^d c_j (j+1)^{-n+1} \, x^j$, which shows $f_n \to 0$ uniformly on $[0,1]$. Now let $f$ be an arbitrary continuous function. Given $\ve>0$, use the Weierstrass approximation theorem to find a polynomial $g$ with $g(0)=0$ such that $\|f-g\|_{\infty} < \ve$. Define the sequence $g_n$ similarly, with $g_1=g$. By the special case above $g_n \to 0$ uniformly on $[0,1]$, so there is an $N \geq 1$ such that $\| g_n \|_{\infty} < \ve$ whenever $n>N$. For any $0 < x \leq 1$, the inequality $$ |f_n(x)-g_n(x)| \leq \frac{1}{x} \int_0^x |f_{n-1}(t)-g_{n-1}(t)| \, dt \leq \| f_{n-1}-g_{n-1} \|_{\infty} $$ gives $\|f_n -g_n\|_{\infty} \leq \| f_{n-1}-g_{n-1} \|_{\infty}$, which in particular shows $\| f_n -g_n \|_{\infty} \leq \| f_1-g_1 \|_{\infty} < \ve$ for all $n$. Thus, $\| f_n \|_{\infty} \leq \| f_n-g_n \|_{\infty}+ \| g_n \|_{\infty} < 2\ve$ whenever $n>N$.
