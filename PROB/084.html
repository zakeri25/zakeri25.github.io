Suppose $f:\M_n(\CC) \to \CC$ is a non-constant function such that $f(AB)=f(A)f(B)$ for all $A,B \in \M_n(\CC)$. Show that $f(A)=0$ if and only if $A$ is singular.

To verify that $f$ vanishes on all singular matrices, first prove that for each $1 \leq r \leq n-1$ the restriction of $f$ to the set of matrices of rank $r$ is either everywhere zero or nowhere zero.

Choose any $A$ with $f(A) \neq 0$ and use $f(A)=f(AI)=f(A)f(I)$ to obtain $f(I)=1$. Choose any $A$ with $f(A) \neq 1$ and use $f(O)=f(OA)=f(O)f(A)$ to obtain $f(O)=0$. If $A$ is invertible, it follows that $f(A)f(A^{-1})=1$, so $f(A) \neq 0$. It remains to prove that $f$ vanishes on every singular matrix.

To this end, we first make an observation: Let $A \in \M_n(\CC)$ and $\rank(A)=r$. By performing elementary row and column operations, we see that there are invertible matrices $P,Q \in \M_n(\CC)$ such that $$ PAQ = \begin{bmatrix} I_{r\times r} & O \\ O & O \end{bmatrix}. $$ This shows that the restriction of $f$ to the set of matrices of rank $r$ is either everywhere zero or nowhere zero. Now suppose $A \neq O$ is singular and $f(A) \neq 0$. We may as well assume that $1 \leq r=\rank(A) \leq n-1$ is smallest among all such matrices. From the above observation, $f$ is non-vanishing on all matrices of rank $r$. Choose $B \in \M_n(\CC)$ with $\rank(B)=r$ such that the image of $x \mapsto Bx$ meets the kernel of $x \mapsto Ax$ non-trivially. Then $\rank(AB) < r$, so $f(A)f(B)=f(AB)=0$ by minimality of $r$, a contradiction.

Remark. Such multiplicative function $f$ is not necessarily related to determinants. For example, when $n=1$, take any discontinuous solution $g:\RR \to \RR$ of Cauchy's functional equation $g(t+s)=g(t)+g(s)$ and set $$ f(z)= \begin{cases} \exp(g(\log|z|)) & \text{if} \ z \neq 0 \\ 0 & \text{if} \ z=0. \end{cases} $$
