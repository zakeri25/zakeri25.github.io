Let $G$ be a group of order $|G|=pqr$, where $p, q, r$ are primes with $p < q < r$. Show that $G$ has a normal subgroup of order $qr$. In particular, $G$ is not simple.

Consider a $q$-Sylow subgroup $H$ and an $r$-Sylow subgroup $K$ of $G$ and check that one of $H, K$ must be normal, so $HK$ is a subgroup of order $qr$.

Consider a $q$-Sylow subgroup $H \cong \ZZ_q$ and an $r$-Sylow subgroup $K \cong \ZZ_r$. Evidently $H \cap K = \{ e \}$, so $|HK|=qr$. It suffices to show that one of $H,K$ is normal in $G$, for then $HK$ will be a subgroup of $G$ of order $qr$, necessarily normal since $[G:HK]=p$ is the smallest prime factor of $|G|$. The number $n_q$ of subgroups of $G$ conjugate to $H$ is congruent to $1$ (mod $q$) and divides $pr$, so $n_q \in \{ 1, r, pr \}$. Similarly, the number $n_r$ of subgroups of $G$ conjugate to $K$ is congruent to $1$ (mod $r$) and divides $pq$, so $n_r \in \{ 1, pq \}$. Suppose $n_q=pr, n_r=pq$. As any two conjugate subgroups intersect along $\{ e \}$ only, this would give rise to $pr(q-1)$ elements of order $q$ and $pq(r-1)$ elements of order $r$ in $G$, which is impossible since $pr(q-1)+pq(r-1)>pqr$. If $n_q=r, n_r=pq$, we would similarly end up with $r(q-1)$ elements of order $q$ and $pq(r-1)$ elements of order $r$ in $G$, which again is impossible since $r(q-1)+pq(r-1)>pqr$. Thus, either $n_q$ or $n_r$ must be $1$, i.e., either $H$ or $K$ must be normal in $G$.

Remark. We can show that the $r$-Sylow subgroup $K$ is always normal. If not, $[G:N(K)]=n_r=pq$ gives $|N(K)|=r$ so $N(K)=K$. On the other hand, $H$ must be normal by the above argument, so the action of $K$ on $H$ given by $k \mapsto khk^{-1}$ induces a homomorphism $K \to \Aut(H)$. This homomorphism must be trivial since $|K|=r$ and $|\Aut(H)|=\phi(q)=q-1$. It follows that elements of $H,K$ commute and in particular $H \subset N(K)=K$, a contradiction.
