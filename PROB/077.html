Fix $a>0$ and let $S$ be the set of all sequences $\{ \ve_n \}$ of $0$'s and $1$'s such that $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \ve_n/n=a$. Show that $S$ is uncountable.

Pick a (canonical) infinite sequence $A$ in $S$ and define an injection $2^A \hookrightarrow S$.

Clearly $S=S_a$ can be identified with the collection of all subsets $A \subset \NN$ with $\sum_{n \in A} 1/n=a$. For each $X \subset \NN$ with $\sum_{n \in X} 1/n=\infty$ define the set $A_a(X) \subset X$ of integers $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$ inductively by the condition that each $n_k$ is the smallest element of $X$ for which $\sum_{j=1}^k 1/n_j < a$. Clearly $A_a(X) \in S_a$ and both $A_a(X)$ and $X \sm A_a(X)$ are infinite. We prove $S_a$ is uncountable by injecting $2^{A_a(\NN)}$ into it: For each subset $B \subset A_a(\NN)$ let $b:=\sum_{n \in B} 1/n$ and consider the disjoint union $$ Y(B):=(A_a(\NN) \sm B) \cup A_b(\NN \sm A_a(\NN)) \in S_a. $$ The assignment $B \mapsto Y(B)$ is easily seen to be injective.
